You made it safely!

But now you may be asking yourself - well I didn’t really need to use a go-link there. Instead I could have just copy pasted the link from the text and that would be even faster than typing. That’s true, but let’s consider another scenario.

Let’s say that you cannot ever remember how to undo the commit in Git. You might have googled this a couple of times already and it would be nice to save the page that contains a correct solution if you need it again.

This is where the go-links are handy. You can create a simple to memorize pattern like volfee.link/git-undo and relate it to https://stackoverflow.com/questions/927358/how-do-i-undo-the-most-recent-local-commits-in-git. That way whenever you find a typo in code seconds after making the commit, you may quickly type volfee.link/git-undo and immediately get the solution on how to fix the problem.

But this gets even better than that. Given that go-links are not local to your machine, but are shared within your organization (or like volfee-links globally). Whenever your colleague commits too early you may tell him to check out git-undo go-link and they as well will be able to access the same stack overflow thread. Go-links are really powerful for easily shareable aliases for web resources like documents, slide decks, tools and project pages that may not be easily accessible due to long URLs.

If you type volfee.link/register into your browser, I will show you how easy it is to create a new go-link.
